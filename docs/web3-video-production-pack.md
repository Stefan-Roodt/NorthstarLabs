# Web3 Foundations video production pack

This pack turns the fourteen scripted video lessons in
`drizzle/0018_stefan_web3_foundations.sql` into a consistent short-form series.
The lesson transcript is the approved narration and caption source.

## Format for every six-minute video

- `00:00–00:25` — open with the learner problem or a concrete misconception.
- `00:25–01:20` — name the lesson outcome and introduce the core model.
- `01:20–03:25` — explain the model with one animated worked example.
- `03:25–04:35` — show the boundary, failure mode, or “reality check.”
- `04:35–05:25` — pause on the application prompt while the presenter models a first step.
- `05:25–06:00` — recap three takeaways and point to the next action.

Read at a calm conversational pace. Allow visual pauses after important distinctions,
rather than filling every second with narration.

## Series look and sound

- Use a dark navy canvas, warm white type, Stefan academy purple accents, and one
  safety amber reserved for warnings.
- Keep one visual idea on screen at a time. Use diagrams, labelled cards, and
  interface simulations rather than stock “crypto coin” footage.
- Show full URLs only for official sources. Never show a real recovery phrase,
  private key, wallet balance, or live transaction approval.
- Record at 1080p, 16:9, 25 or 30 fps, with clean speech and no background music
  beneath technical explanations.
- Burn in no captions. Upload a clean video and use the platform transcript as
  accessible captions and a readable alternative.

## Lesson-by-lesson visual brief

### 1. Web3 without the hype

Open on three cards: rules, assets, trust. Animate Web2 control into one platform,
then distribute selected controls across protocol, users, and governance. End with
the three-question inspection lens.

### 2. How a blockchain creates shared state

Build one transaction across five beats: proposed change, signature, block, hash
link, consensus. Corrupt one old record and turn every later hash link red. Close
with the certificate example that separates an immutable record from a truthful
issuer.

### 3. Wallets, keys, addresses, and signatures

Use a cutaway wallet diagram: public address outside, private key and recovery
phrase inside the security boundary. Animate a message entering, a signature
leaving, and the secret never leaving. Put the safety rule on screen for a full
five seconds.

### 4. Transactions, gas, and finality

Turn a transaction into a labelled receipt. Move it through pending, included,
confirmed, and finalised states. Branch once into a failed execution that still
shows computation and a fee.

### 5. Smart contracts and the EVM

Use an escrow state machine with deposit, release, refund, and invalid transition.
Show several nodes producing the same next state. Add an oracle at the system
boundary to demonstrate what a contract cannot know by itself.

### 6. Tokens: fungible, non-fungible, and misunderstood

Compare identical units with individually numbered records. Split the screen into
“on-chain fact” and “off-chain promise,” then place copyright, physical title,
metadata availability, and issuer honesty on the promise side.

### 7. Dapps, oracles, and content addressing

Trace one click through interface, wallet, contract, oracle, indexer, and storage.
Place a trust label on each arrow. For IPFS, replace a server location with a
content fingerprint, then show that persistence still needs providers.

### 8. Layer 1, layer 2, sidechains, and bridges

Use stacked lanes for execution, data, proof, settlement, and exit. Compare a
rollup and a sidechain without naming commercial projects. Animate a bridge lock
and representation, then highlight the new verification boundary.

### 9. DAOs and governance beyond token voting

Build the governance stack from membership to accountability. Run a community
learning-fund proposal through discussion, vote, multisignature execution, and
reporting. Show low participation and concentrated voting as visible failure
states.

### 10. Decentralised identity and verifiable credentials

Animate issuer, holder, and verifier as three separate roles. Move a minimum
credential between them, verify its integrity, and then pause at the verifier’s
independent trust decision. Remove unnecessary personal attributes to demonstrate
data minimisation.

### 11. Wallet security: stop, inspect, verify

Stage a simulated phishing message. Freeze the screen at urgency, lookalike domain,
and broad approval. Apply the SIV routine one step at a time. End with a clean
six-item pre-signing checklist.

### 12. DeFi, stablecoins, and layered risk

Stack contract, oracle, market, governance, custody, bridge, interface, and legal
risk around one fictional product. Use neutral diagrams and no price charts. Make
the source of yield and the stablecoin support model visible.

### 13. When a blockchain is the wrong tool

Run a fashionable idea through the five-question necessity test. Compare it with
a signed database record, append-only log, and conventional escrow. Celebrate the
decision to remove blockchain when the baseline wins.

### 14. Design a responsible Web3 product

Fill the ten-part product canvas in sequence. Shrink the proposed on-chain data to
the minimum safe record. Finish with success measures—completion, cost, error,
recovery, comprehension—and explicitly cross out token price.

## Course trailer: 45 seconds

> Web3 is bigger than token prices—and more complicated than “trustless” slogans.
> In Web3 Foundations, you will learn how blockchains create shared state, what
> wallets and signatures really do, how smart contracts and tokens work, and where
> dapps still depend on people and organisations. You will inspect a real
> transaction without spending money, threat-model a suspicious wallet request,
> evaluate DeFi and bridge risks, and design a responsible product of your own.
> No investment pitch. No real funds required. Just the mental models, safety
> habits, and practical judgement you need to move from curious learner to
> credible Web3 builder.
